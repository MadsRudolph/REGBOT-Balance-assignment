\section{Position Controller (Task 4)}
\label{sec:position}

\subsection{Plant Transfer Function}
With the balance loop of Task~\ref{sec:balance} and the velocity loop of Task~\ref{sec:velocity} closed, the plant seen by the outermost controller is the closed-loop velocity system cascaded with a free integrator (wheel position is the integral of wheel velocity):
\begin{equation}
    G_{pos,\mathrm{outer}}(s) \;=\; \frac{X(s)}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}(s)} \;\approx\; \frac{1}{s}\cdot T_{\mathrm{vel}}(s).
\end{equation}
The plant is identified by linearising the Simulink model from the output of the \texttt{Kppos\_gain} block to the \texttt{x\_position} output (port~3 of the \texttt{/robot with balance} subsystem) with \texttt{Kppos~=~0}. The identified plant has $0$ RHP poles, one pole at the origin (the free integrator), and all remaining poles in the left half plane --- confirming that the inner loops have successfully stabilised the open-loop unstable dynamics.

\subsection{Controller Design}
Because the plant is already Type-1, a pure proportional controller is sufficient to drive the steady-state error to a step position reference to zero --- no additional I-term is needed. The cascade-separation rule (outer crossover at least a few times below the inner) gives an upper bound on $\omega_{c,\mathrm{pos}}$ set by $\omega_{c,\mathrm{vel}} = 1\,\mathrm{rad/s}$.

Three candidate crossovers were evaluated against the mission specs (peak velocity $> 0.7\,\mathrm{m/s}$ on a 2\,m move, mission window 10\,s):

The chosen separation ratio $\omega_{c,\mathrm{pos}}/\omega_{c,\mathrm{vel}} = 0.6$ is aggressive (1.67$\times$, instead of the conservative 5$\times$ rule), but the phase-margin cushion at $\omega_{c} = 0.2$ was large enough to absorb the push.

At $\omega_c = 0.6\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ the plant phase for the redesigned cascade is $\angle G_{pos,\mathrm{outer}}(j\omega_c) = -122.9^{\circ}$, giving a phase-lead requirement of
\begin{equation}
    \phi_{\mathrm{Lead}} = -180^{\circ} + \gamma_M - \angle G_{pos,\mathrm{outer}}(j\omega_c) = +2.85^{\circ},
\end{equation}
which would correspond to an ideal Lead with $\tau_d = 0.083\,\mathrm{s}$. As in the original design, a pure Lead $(\tau_d s + 1)$ is an improper transfer function and cannot be realised by a Simulink \texttt{Transfer Fcn} block, so it is omitted. The $\approx 3^{\circ}$ phase-margin sacrifice is accepted:
\begin{equation}
    C_{pos}(s) = K_P = 0.5411, \qquad \gamma_M \approx 57^{\circ}.
\end{equation}

The loop gain is set by $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$:
\begin{equation}
    |G_{pos,\mathrm{outer}}(j\omega_c)| = 1.848, \qquad K_P = \frac{1}{1.848} = 0.5411.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Comments}
Test~4 is the cleanest run of the campaign: the robot reaches $1.964\,\mathrm{m}$ ($3.6\,\mathrm{cm}$ short of target), there is no overshoot, and --- distinctively --- there is no late-period limit cycle once the robot arrives. The hardware peak velocity ($0.79\,\mathrm{m/s}$) is comfortably above the $0.7\,\mathrm{m/s}$ mission floor and closely matches the linear-model prediction of $0.75\,\mathrm{m/s}$. The forward-tilt excursion during acceleration is $+17.3^{\circ}$, well inside the small-angle range where the linearised design assumptions are accurate, and the motor voltage peaks at $4.95\,\mathrm{V}$ --- $62\,\%$ of the saturation budget.

\paragraph{What the tighter inner loop contributes on Test~4.} The position-loop command \texttt{topos=2} produces a smoothly rising $v_\mathrm{ref}$ from the outer P controller, not a step input to the inner loops. The aggressive corner-entry voltage transients seen in Test~3b (Section~\ref{sec:velocity}) do not appear: the inner PI is never asked to track a $0.8\,\mathrm{m/s}$ step. What \emph{is} visible is the benefit of the tighter balance loop --- the robot leans less during acceleration ($17.3^{\circ}$ peak versus $25^{\circ}$ in the original campaign), and the tilt standard deviation over the run is $2.93^{\circ}$ (versus $5.18^{\circ}$ previously). A tighter balance loop means less over-tilt during acceleration, which in turn means less impulsive forward thrust; peak hardware velocity is therefore slightly lower than in the earlier, looser campaign ($0.79$ versus $1.01\,\mathrm{m/s}$), but still above the $0.7$ spec. The redesign trades excess acceleration for much cleaner tracking near the target.

\paragraph{No late limit cycle.} In the original campaign, after reaching $\approx 1.97\,\mathrm{m}$ the robot drifted back and entered a small-amplitude limit cycle (pitch $\pm 10^{\circ}$, $v_\mathrm{ref}$ saturated at $\pm 0.5\,\mathrm{m/s}$) driven by the interaction of a $\approx 0.8^{\circ}$ tilt-offset bias, wheel static friction, and the looser balance loop. The redesigned controller suppresses this: the balance loop is now fast enough to regulate the residual bias without triggering the stick-slip dead zone at the wheels, and the run ends with the robot stationary at $1.964\,\mathrm{m}$ and pitch near zero. The $3.6\,\mathrm{cm}$ shortfall is a static error from the same tilt-offset mechanism that drives the Test~3a drift (Section~\ref{sec:task2_hw}); it is $\approx 3\times$ smaller than in the earlier campaign, consistent with the tighter balance loop integrating the bias less aggressively.

\paragraph{Small-angle linearity holds.} The peak forward tilt of $17.3^{\circ}$ is close to but inside the region where the small-angle linearisation the design relies on is accurate. The corresponding earlier-campaign tilt of $25^{\circ}$ sat firmly in the non-linear regime and depended on non-modelled gyro damping to return cleanly. The redesigned controller keeps the Task~4 mission within the region where the design's assumptions hold.

Overall the cascade of four redesigned controllers performs the full 2\,m position-step mission in simulation and on the physical robot, meeting every listed specification with comfortable margin.
