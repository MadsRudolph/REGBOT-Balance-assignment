\section{Velocity Controller (Task 3)}
\label{sec:velocity}

\subsection{Plant Transfer Function}
With the balance loop of Task~\ref{sec:balance} closed, the outer plant is identified by linearising the Simulink model from the output of the \texttt{Kpvel\_gain} block (which produces the tilt reference $\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}$) to the wheel-velocity filter output \texttt{/wheel\_vel\_filter}. This gives $G_{vel,\mathrm{outer}}(s) = \theta_{\mathrm{ref}} \to v$. The identified plant has zero RHP poles (the closed balance loop has stabilised the pendulum mode) but a real RHP zero at approximately $+8.67\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, which reflects the non-minimum-phase physics of balancing: the wheels must first roll backward for the body to tilt forward.

The RHP zero fundamentally bounds the crossover of the velocity loop through the rule of thumb $\omega_c \leq z/5$, i.e.\ $\omega_c \lesssim 1.73\,\mathrm{rad/s}$.

\subsection{Controller Design}
A pure PI controller is used. The outer loop is made substantially slower than the inner balance loop so the closed balance dynamics appear as a high-bandwidth unity gain at $\omega_{c,\mathrm{vel}}$. The design specifications are listed in Table~\ref{tab:task3_specs}.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{l|c|l}
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Reason} \\
        \hline
        $\omega_c$ & $1\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ & Safely below the RHP zero at $+8.67\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ (rule $\omega_c \leq z/5$) \\
        $\gamma_M$ & $\geq 60^\circ$      & Robust phase margin (specification) \\
        $N_i$      & $3$                 & PI zero placed at $\omega_c/N_i$ below crossover \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Task 3 design specifications.}
    \label{tab:task3_specs}
\end{table}

The PI zero is placed at $\tau_i = N_i/\omega_c = 3\,\mathrm{s}$. At $\omega_c = 1\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ the plant phase is $\angle G_{vel,\mathrm{outer}}(j\omega_c) = -101.8^\circ$ and the PI contributes $-\arctan(1/N_i) = -18.4^\circ$, giving
\begin{equation}
    \phi_{\mathrm{Lead}} = -180^{\circ} + \gamma_M - \angle G_{vel,\mathrm{outer}}(j\omega_c) - \angle C_{PI}(j\omega_c) = -0.2^{\circ},
\end{equation}
so no Lead is required --- the $60^\circ$ phase-margin spec is met by the PI alone. The proportional gain is obtained from $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$:
\begin{equation}
    K_P = \frac{1}{|C_{PI}(j\omega_c)\,G_{vel,\mathrm{outer}}(j\omega_c)|} = 0.162.
\end{equation}
The velocity controller is
\begin{equation}
    C_{vel}(s) = K_P\cdot\frac{\tau_i s + 1}{\tau_i s} = 0.162\cdot\frac{3s + 1}{3s}.
    \label{eq:cvel}
\end{equation}

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{l|c|c}
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Target} & \textbf{Achieved} \\
        \hline
        $K_P$              & --                    & $0.1616$ \\
        $\tau_i$           & --                    & $3.000\,\mathrm{s}$ \\
        $\omega_c$         & $1\,\mathrm{rad/s}$   & $1.00\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ \\
        $\gamma_M$         & $\geq 60^\circ$       & $64.2^\circ$ \\
        Gain margin        & --                    & $7.84\,\mathrm{dB}$ \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Task 3 design summary.}
    \label{tab:task3_results}
\end{table}

\subsection{REGBOT Experiments}

\subsubsection{Test 3a --- Zero Velocity (balance in place)}
Covered in Section~\ref{sec:balance} (Task~2 hardware). With \texttt{vel=0, bal=1, log=15 : time=10} the robot held balance for the full 10\,s with $0.343\,\mathrm{m}$ of drift, inside the $0.5\,\mathrm{m}$ specification.

\subsubsection{Test 3b --- Square Run at 0.8 m/s}
The square run was performed at the assignment-specified $0.8\,\mathrm{m/s}$ using the mission
\begin{verbatim}
vel=0, bal=1, log=15 : time=2
vel=0.8                       : dist=1
vel=0.8, tr=0.2               : turn=90
(repeated three more times)
vel=0
\end{verbatim}
which commands four $1\,\mathrm{m}$ straights with $0.2\,\mathrm{m}$-radius corner arcs between them. The robot completed all four sides and three turns without falling. The XY trajectory is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:task3_hw_xy} and the corresponding time series in Figure~\ref{fig:task3_hw_ts}.

The measured side length was $\approx 1.42\,\mathrm{m}$, larger than the intended $1.00\,\mathrm{m}$. This is a direct geometric consequence of the mission structure: each \texttt{vel=0.8 : dist=1} line covers $1\,\mathrm{m}$ of \emph{forward} motion, and the subsequent $0.2\,\mathrm{m}$ turn arc adds another $\pi\cdot 0.2 / 2 \approx 0.31\,\mathrm{m}$ of x-(or y-)displacement per corner, for $\approx 1.31\,\mathrm{m}$ of axis-aligned travel between corners. The deviation from an ideal square is also why the end-pose ($-0.21\,\mathrm{m}, +0.23\,\mathrm{m}$) does not coincide exactly with the start. A first attempt at $0.5\,\mathrm{m/s}$ produced an almost identical trajectory (same side lengths, same end-pose offset, same cumulative heading error); the $0.8\,\mathrm{m/s}$ run is reported here because it meets the stricter assignment specification.

\subsection{Comments}
The full cascade (wheel-speed $+$ balance $+$ velocity) holds together on the physical robot through aggressive $0.2\,\mathrm{m}$-radius corners at the full $0.8\,\mathrm{m/s}$ spec. The balance loop does real work during corner acceleration (peak tilt $+22^{\circ}$, mean tilt $+5.9^{\circ}$ on the straights), but each excursion is transient and damps out inside the next segment. Motor voltage peaks at $4.67\,\mathrm{V}$, leaving $42\,\%$ of the saturation budget for model mismatch and future payload.

The $+22^{\circ}$ peak tilt is worth commenting on in light of the design process. At design time the Simulink simulation warned that a pure $0.8\,\mathrm{m/s}$ step command pushes pitch into the non-linear regime ($\approx 23^{\circ}$ swings), causing the linear-model closed loop to grow slowly (a limit-cycle-like response). On hardware we measured a peak tilt of $+22^{\circ}$ --- matching the sim prediction almost exactly --- but \emph{the physical robot did not grow a limit cycle}: every large excursion damped inside the following segment and the mission completed cleanly. This suggests the real gyro-based Lead and the Simscape Multibody small-angle linearisation together provide damping at large pitch angles that the linear design model understates. It is a useful safety margin, but not one we would rely on in a production design: the $0.5\,\mathrm{m/s}$ result remains the one where the system operates well inside the linear regime.
