\section{Balance Controller (Task 2)}
\label{sec:balance}

\paragraph{Why it is hard.} The plant is open-loop \emph{unstable} (one RHP pole = the inverted-pendulum falling mode). A standard PI-Lead cannot stabilise it, because no positive gain makes the Nyquist plot of $G_{tilt}$ encircle $(-1,0)$ counter-clockwise. So we use \emph{Method~2} from Lecture~10: a sign flip and a ``post-integrator'' reshape the plant into a stabilisable form, and the usual PI-Lead recipe then runs on top.

\paragraph{Plant.} Linearising \texttt{vel\_ref} $\to$ tilt with Task~1 closed gives a 7th-order $G_{tilt}(s)$ with one RHP pole at $\approx +8.9\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ and one RHP zero (the non-minimum-phase ``wheels-roll-backward-first'' signature). With $Z = 0$ and $P = 1$ the Nyquist criterion demands $N = -1$ (exactly one CCW encirclement).

\paragraph{Method~2 pre-treatment.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep,label=\textbf{M\arabic*.}]
    \item \textbf{Sign of $K_{PS}$.} $G_{tilt}$ has DC gain $> 0$, and positive gain only scales the Nyquist plot radially, so it cannot rotate the curve to the left of $(-1,0)$. This means $\text{sign}(K_{PS}) = -1$, and the minus sign is absorbed into the post-integrator below.
    \item \textbf{Post-integrator at the peak of $|G_{tilt}|$.} The plant's magnitude rises into a resonance peak at $\omega_{\mathrm{peak}} = 8.03\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, and we place the PI zero there: $\tau_{i,\mathrm{post}} = 1/\omega_{\mathrm{peak}} = 0.1245\,\mathrm{s}$. The reshaped plant
    \[
        G_{tilt,\mathrm{post}}(s) = -\,C_{PI,\mathrm{post}}(s)\,G_{tilt}(s)
    \]
    has \emph{monotonically decreasing} magnitude and a Nyquist that makes exactly one CCW encirclement of $(-1,0)$, which is the visual proof that Method~2 worked.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Standard recipe on $G_{tilt,\mathrm{post}}$.} We now apply R2--R5 from §\ref{sec:architecture}.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{rl}
$\omega_c,\ \gamma_M,\ N_i$ & $15\,\mathrm{rad/s},\ 60^{\circ},\ 3$ \\
PI zero: $\tau_i$ & $0.200\,\mathrm{s}$ \\
$\angle G_{tilt,\mathrm{post}}(j15)$ & $-135.1^{\circ}$ \\
$\phi_{PI}$ & $-18.4^{\circ}$ \\
Required $\phi_{\mathrm{Lead}} = -180^{\circ} + \gamma_M - \angle G - \phi_{PI}$ & $+33.5^{\circ}$ -- Lead \emph{is} needed \\
$\tau_d = \tan(33.5^{\circ}) / 15$ & $0.0442\,\mathrm{s}$ \\
$|L|_{\mathrm{unscaled}}$ at $\omega_c$ & $0.833$ \\
$K_P$ & $\mathbf{1.1999}$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent We realise the Lead through the gyro signal, which directly measures $\dot{\theta} = s\theta$, as $\tau_d \cdot \mathrm{gyro} + \theta$. This is the \emph{ideal} Lead $(\tau_d s + 1)$ without the filter pole $1/(\alpha \tau_d s + 1)$: the noise problem that an ordinary Lead guards against is gone, because the differentiation is measured rather than numerical.

\[
C_{\mathrm{tilt}}(s) = -1.1999 \cdot \frac{0.1245s + 1}{0.1245s} \cdot \frac{0.2s + 1}{0.2s} \cdot (0.0442s + 1).
\]
\noindent The Simulink \texttt{Tilt\_Controller} subsystem implements this stack in series, with $\tau_d \cdot \mathrm{gyro}$ summed onto $\mathrm{pitch}$ on the measurement side so the Lead has no derivative kick on $\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}$.
\paragraph{Checking the loop (R6).} \texttt{margin(L\_tilt)}: $\omega_c = 15.00$, $\gamma_M = 60.00^{\circ}$, $GM = -5.58\,\mathrm{dB}$ (a negative GM is the \emph{lower} bound on $|K|$ for unstable plants), $0$ RHP closed-loop poles. The linear IC release from $\theta_0 = 10^{\circ}$ settles in $\mathbf{1.34\,s}$ with $\sim 6.6^{\circ}$ undershoot, the RHP-zero signature.

\paragraph{Hardware (Test~3a).} \texttt{vel=0, bal=1, log=15 : time=10}: the robot was held upright, balance enabled, then released. It held the full $10\,\mathrm{s}$ with a drift of $\mathbf{0.343\,m}$ (spec $\leq 0.5$ \checkmark), which confirms the closed loop is stable on the physical plant. \emph{Firmware:} \texttt{[cbal] kp} must be entered as $\mathbf{-1.1999}$, because the firmware does not absorb Method~2's sign internally (a positive $K_P$ caused a runaway in the first tests).
