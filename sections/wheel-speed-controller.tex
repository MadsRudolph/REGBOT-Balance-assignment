\section{Wheel Speed Controller (Task 1)}
\label{sec:wheel-speed}

\subsection{Plant Transfer Function}
The plant used for the wheel-speed controller is the voltage-to-velocity transfer function identified on Day~5 from black-box measurements, with the robot lying on its side so the tilt dynamics are decoupled:
\begin{equation}
    G_{vel}(s) = \frac{13.34}{s + 35.71}
    \label{eq:gvel_day5}
\end{equation}
The DC gain is $0.374\,\mathrm{(m/s)/V}$, the time constant is $\tau = 28\,\mathrm{ms}$, and the only pole lies at $-35.71\,\mathrm{rad/s}$.

\subsection{Controller Design}
A PI controller is selected so that the steady-state error to a step velocity reference is zero. The inner loop is designed to be fast so that the balance controller in the outer loop can treat the velocity dynamics as nearly instantaneous. The design specifications and chosen parameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:task1_specs}.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{l|c|l}
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Reason} \\
        \hline
        $\omega_c$   & $30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ & Fast inner loop, below the plant pole at $35.71\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ \\
        $\gamma_M$   & $\geq 60^\circ$      & Robust phase margin (specification) \\
        $N_i$        & $3$                  & PI zero placed at $\omega_c/N_i$ below crossover \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Task 1 design specifications.}
    \label{tab:task1_specs}
\end{table}

Using the phase-balance equation at $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, the controller provides a phase contribution of
\[
    \phi_{PI} = \arctan(N_i) - 90^\circ = \arctan(3) - 90^\circ \approx -18.4^\circ,
\]
and the plant provides
\[
    \angle G_{vel}(j\omega_c) = -\arctan(30/35.71) \approx -40.0^\circ,
\]
for a total open-loop phase of $-58.4^\circ$ at $\omega_c$, giving a phase margin well above the $60^\circ$ target. The PI zero is placed at $\omega_c/N_i = 10\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, i.e.\ $\tau_i = 0.1\,\mathrm{s}$. The proportional gain is chosen so that $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$:
\[
    K_p = \frac{1}{|G_{vel}(j\omega_c)|\cdot|(\tau_i j\omega_c + 1)/(\tau_i j\omega_c)|} \approx 3.31.
\]
The resulting controller is
\begin{equation}
    C_{wv}(s) = K_p \cdot \frac{\tau_i s + 1}{\tau_i s}
    = 3.31 \cdot \frac{0.1s + 1}{0.1s}.
    \label{eq:cwv}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Simulink Implementation}
The designed gains are assigned to the Simulink starter model variables \texttt{Kpwv = 3.31} and \texttt{tiwv = 0.10}\,s, replacing the default sample values provided with the model. This ensures the inner velocity loop in the Simscape Multibody model uses the Task 1 design for all subsequent experiments.

\subsection{Comments}
The achieved phase margin of $121.6^{\circ}$ is far above the $60^{\circ}$ specification, making the controller very robust but slightly conservative. The design is intentionally conservative to make the inner loop predictable for the outer balance controller.

\paragraph{Discrepancy with the Day~5 model.} The hardware test reveals a quantitative mismatch with the Day~5 linear plant that bears on the validity of the whole cascade design, so it is worth unpacking.

Holding $0.3\,\mathrm{m/s}$ in steady state required $\approx 1.8\,\mathrm{V}$, whereas the Day~5 model $G_{vel,\mathrm{day5}}(s) = 13.34/(s + 35.71)$ predicts $0.3/0.374 \approx 0.8\,\mathrm{V}$ --- a factor-of-$2.25$ discrepancy. Two candidate explanations, not mutually exclusive:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Linear DC-gain error.} The true plant DC gain is $\approx 0.167\,\mathrm{(m/s)/V}$ rather than $0.374\,\mathrm{(m/s)/V}$. The Day~5 identification was done with the robot rolling freely on a flat surface, which may have understated the load torque the motor sees when the robot is balancing on its wheels in its normal configuration (full mass, proper tyre contact patch).
    \item \textbf{Non-linear friction (Coulomb $+$ viscous).} The plant dynamics agree with Day~5, but a roughly constant $\approx 1\,\mathrm{V}$ overhead is needed to overcome bearing, gearbox, tyre, and air friction. A single-point steady-state measurement cannot distinguish this from (1) --- we would need velocity--voltage curves at several operating points to fit an affine model of the form $V_{ss}(v) = V_{\mathrm{coulomb}} + v/K_{dc}$.
\end{enumerate}

The rise time to $90\,\%$ of target was $0.329\,\mathrm{s}$ on hardware versus $\approx 80\,\mathrm{ms}$ for a first-order closed loop at $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$. Part of this is explained by the measured wheel-velocity estimator delay of $\approx 5\,\mathrm{ms}$ and the $15\,\mathrm{ms}$ log-sampling interval (the internal control loop runs at $1\,\mathrm{kHz}$), but a real slowdown is likely also present: the effective closed-loop bandwidth on the physical robot appears closer to $7\text{--}10\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ than the designed $30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$.

\paragraph{Why we keep the Day~5 model.} We did not re-identify the plant, for three reasons:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The PI integrator in the wheel-speed loop absorbs the voltage overhead as an input-referred disturbance, driving the steady-state velocity error to $\approx 1\,\%$ on hardware (essentially zero).
    \item Task~1 was designed with $121.6^{\circ}$ phase-margin cushion --- far more than the $60^{\circ}$ specification required. Even at a reduced effective bandwidth of $7\text{--}10\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, the loop phase margin remains well above $60^{\circ}$, so stability is not in question.
    \item The outer loops (Tasks~2--4) are designed against $G_{tilt}$ and $G_{vel,\mathrm{outer}}$, which are obtained by \texttt{linearize()} on the \emph{full} Simulink model (with the Task~1 gains in place and the true Simscape Multibody dynamics), not against $G_{vel,\mathrm{day5}}$ directly. The Day~5 model therefore only directly informs Task~1; its errors propagate weakly to the outer designs.
\end{itemize}

The practical consequence is that all four controllers pass their hardware specifications with the designs derived from the Day~5 plant. If future work required tighter margins or faster inner-loop response, a re-identification at several operating points with the robot in balance configuration would refine the plant and allow a more aggressive Task~1 design.

The left and right motors agree to $0.36\,\%$ in mean velocity, so no per-wheel gain trim is needed.
