\section{Wheel Speed Controller (Task 1)}
\label{sec:wheel-speed}

\subsection{Plant Transfer Function}
The inner loop's voltage-to-velocity plant was identified on Day~5 from a black-box swept-sine experiment \emph{with the robot rolling on the floor on training wheels}, giving a single-pole fit
\begin{equation}
    G_{vel}(s) = \frac{2.198}{s + 5.985},
    \label{eq:gvel_day5}
\end{equation}
with DC gain $0.367\,\mathrm{(m/s)/V}$, time constant $\tau = 167\,\mathrm{ms}$, and a single pole at $-5.985\,\mathrm{rad/s}$. This is the operating regime the outer loops will see during the assignment missions, so using it as the Task~1 design plant aligns the inner-loop design with the environment in which it has to work.

\paragraph{Note on a first, discarded identification.} An earlier attempt used a wheels-up identification ($G_{vel} = 13.34/(s+35.71)$, $\tau = 28\,\mathrm{ms}$) as the Task~1 plant. That fit described the motor-and-wheel mechanical pair in free space, not the robot rolling on the floor, and its dominant time constant was roughly six times faster than the true on-floor plant. Section~\ref{sec:task1_hw} explains why this mattered and how the measurement exposed it; the redesign described here is the one committed in the firmware.

\subsection{Controller Design}
A PI controller is selected so that the steady-state error to a step velocity reference is zero. The inner loop is designed to be fast so that the balance controller in the outer loop can treat the velocity dynamics as nearly instantaneous. The design specifications and chosen parameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:task1_specs}.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{l|c|l}
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Reason} \\
        \hline
        $\omega_c$   & $30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ & Fast inner loop, five times above the plant pole at $5.985\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ \\
        $\gamma_M$   & $\geq 60^\circ$      & Robust phase margin (specification) \\
        $N_i$        & $3$                  & PI zero placed at $\omega_c/N_i$ below crossover \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Task 1 design specifications.}
    \label{tab:task1_specs}
\end{table}

Using the phase-balance equation at $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, the PI controller contributes
\[
    \phi_{PI} = \arctan(N_i) - 90^\circ = \arctan(3) - 90^\circ \approx -18.4^\circ,
\]
and the plant
\[
    \angle G_{vel}(j\omega_c) = -\arctan(30/5.985) \approx -78.7^\circ,
\]
for a total open-loop phase of $-97.1^\circ$ at $\omega_c$, giving an open-loop phase margin of $82.9^\circ$ --- comfortably above the $60^\circ$ target. The PI zero is placed at $\omega_c/N_i = 10\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, i.e.\ $\tau_i = 0.100\,\mathrm{s}$. The proportional gain is chosen so that $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$:
\[
    K_p = \frac{1}{|G_{vel}(j\omega_c)|\cdot|(\tau_i j\omega_c + 1)/(\tau_i j\omega_c)|} = \frac{1}{0.0758} \approx 13.20.
\]
The resulting controller is
\begin{equation}
    C_{wv}(s) = K_p \cdot \frac{\tau_i s + 1}{\tau_i s}
    = 13.2037 \cdot \frac{0.1s + 1}{0.1s}.
    \label{eq:cwv}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Simulink Implementation}
The designed gains are assigned to the Simulink starter-model variables \texttt{Kpwv = 13.2037} and \texttt{tiwv = 0.10}\,s via \texttt{regbot\_mg.m}, which Simulink loads through its \texttt{PreLoadFcn}. The inner velocity loop in the Simscape Multibody model therefore uses the Task~1 design for all subsequent experiments, and the corresponding firmware block (\texttt{[cvel]} in \texttt{config/regbot\_group47.ini}) carries the same values on the physical robot.

\subsection{Comments}
The achieved phase margin of $82.9^{\circ}$ is well above the $60^{\circ}$ specification. The PI zero is placed above the plant pole, so the integrator still contributes phase at $\omega_c$ without dropping the loop below its margin target.

\paragraph{How the redesign came about.} The original Task~1 design used the wheels-up identification $G_{vel} = 13.34/(s+35.71)$, which gave a much more conservative controller ($K_p = 3.31$, $\gamma_M = 121.6^{\circ}$). That controller also passed every assignment spec on the bench, but Test~0 exposed a quantitative mismatch: the measured $90\,\%$ rise time was $0.329\,\mathrm{s}$, whereas a first-order closed loop at $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ predicts $\approx 80\,\mathrm{ms}$. The effective on-hardware bandwidth was therefore only $\approx 9\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, about four times below the designed $30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$. The conservative phase-margin cushion of $121^{\circ}$ was absorbing the model mismatch, keeping the closed loop stable but at a much slower effective bandwidth than the design claimed. Re-identifying against the on-floor plant (Eq.~\eqref{eq:gvel_day5}) and recomputing $K_p$ against the same $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ target lifts $K_p$ to $13.2037$. The measured Test~0 rise time with the redesigned controller is $0.012\,\mathrm{s}$, consistent to within the $15\,\mathrm{ms}$ log-sampling interval with the designed bandwidth.

\paragraph{What Test~0 measures and what it does not.} Test~0 is run wheels-up (balance has to be disabled so the robot cannot stay upright), so the steady-state voltage is not directly comparable to the Day~5 on-floor DC-gain prediction --- it describes a different physical load. Using the REGBOT motor parameters (gear ratio $N_G = 9.69$, wheel radius $W_R = 0.03\,\mathrm{m}$, EMF constant $K_{\mathrm{emf}} = 0.0105\,\mathrm{V\,s/rad}$, armature resistance $R_A = 1.65\,\Omega$), rotation at $v = 0.3\,\mathrm{m/s}$ gives
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{\mathrm{motor}} = \frac{v}{W_R}\,N_G = \frac{0.3}{0.03}\cdot 9.69 = 96.9\,\mathrm{rad/s},
    \quad
    V_{\mathrm{BEMF}} = K_{\mathrm{emf}}\,\omega_{\mathrm{motor}} \approx 1.02\,\mathrm{V}.
\end{equation}
The remaining steady-state voltage is accounted for by the $I\!R$ drop across the armature resistance due to the current required to overcome bearing and gearbox friction. The on-floor behaviour of $G_{vel}$ is validated indirectly by Tasks~2--4, where the robot drives on the floor with the wheel-speed PI operating inside the cascade, and each test meets its specification.

\paragraph{Noise/saturation trade-off from the higher gain.} The four-fold increase in $K_p$ compared to the initial conservative design raises the inner PI's response to measurement noise and to sharp step commands from the outer loops. Test~0 itself stays well inside the motor limit ($2.60\,\mathrm{V}$ peak), but Section~\ref{sec:velocity} (Test~3b at $0.8\,\mathrm{m/s}$) shows that corner entries --- where the velocity outer loop issues sharp $v_\mathrm{ref}$ steps --- now drive the motor voltage up to $7.31\,\mathrm{V}$, compared to $4.67\,\mathrm{V}$ with the older conservative design. This is the expected bandwidth-versus-saturation-margin trade; both campaigns pass the assignment spec, but the redesigned controller operates with a tighter voltage headroom during aggressive corner commands.

The left and right motors agree to $0.76\,\%$ in mean velocity, so no per-wheel gain trim is needed.
