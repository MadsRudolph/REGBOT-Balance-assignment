\section{Wheel Speed Controller (Task 1)}
\label{sec:wheel-speed}

\subsection{Plant Transfer Function}
The plant used for the wheel-speed controller is the voltage-to-velocity transfer function identified on Day~5 from black-box measurements, with the robot lying on its side so the tilt dynamics are decoupled:
\begin{equation}
    G_{vel}(s) = \frac{13.34}{s + 35.71}
    \label{eq:gvel_day5}
\end{equation}
The DC gain is $0.374\,\mathrm{(m/s)/V}$, the time constant is $\tau = 28\,\mathrm{ms}$, and the only pole lies at $-35.71\,\mathrm{rad/s}$.

\subsection{Controller Design}
A PI controller is selected so that the steady-state error to a step velocity reference is zero. The inner loop is designed to be fast so that the balance controller in the outer loop can treat the velocity dynamics as nearly instantaneous. The design specifications and chosen parameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:task1_specs}.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{l|c|l}
        \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Reason} \\
        \hline
        $\omega_c$   & $30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ & Fast inner loop, below the plant pole at $35.71\,\mathrm{rad/s}$ \\
        $\gamma_M$   & $\geq 60^\circ$      & Robust phase margin (specification) \\
        $N_i$        & $3$                  & PI zero placed at $\omega_c/N_i$ below crossover \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Task 1 design specifications.}
    \label{tab:task1_specs}
\end{table}

Using the phase-balance equation at $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, the controller provides a phase contribution of
\[
    \phi_{PI} = \arctan(N_i) - 90^\circ = \arctan(3) - 90^\circ \approx -18.4^\circ,
\]
and the plant provides
\[
    \angle G_{vel}(j\omega_c) = -\arctan(30/35.71) \approx -40.0^\circ,
\]
for a total open-loop phase of $-58.4^\circ$ at $\omega_c$, giving a phase margin well above the $60^\circ$ target. The PI zero is placed at $\omega_c/N_i = 10\,\mathrm{rad/s}$, i.e.\ $\tau_i = 0.1\,\mathrm{s}$. The proportional gain is chosen so that $|L(j\omega_c)| = 1$:
\[
    K_p = \frac{1}{|G_{vel}(j\omega_c)|\cdot|(\tau_i j\omega_c + 1)/(\tau_i j\omega_c)|} \approx 3.31.
\]
The resulting controller is
\begin{equation}
    C_{wv}(s) = K_p \cdot \frac{\tau_i s + 1}{\tau_i s}
    = 3.31 \cdot \frac{0.1s + 1}{0.1s}.
    \label{eq:cwv}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Simulink Implementation}
The designed gains are assigned to the Simulink starter model variables \texttt{Kpwv = 3.31} and \texttt{tiwv = 0.10}\,s, replacing the default sample values provided with the model. This ensures the inner velocity loop in the Simscape Multibody model uses the Task 1 design for all subsequent experiments.

\subsection{Comments}
The achieved phase margin of $121.6^{\circ}$ is far above the $60^{\circ}$ specification, making the controller very robust but slightly conservative. The design is intentionally conservative to make the inner loop predictable for the outer balance controller.

\paragraph{Operating-regime caveat for Test~0.} Test~0 was run with the robot lying on its back and the wheels spinning freely in the air (Balance had to be disabled for this isolated wheel-speed test, so the robot could not stay upright). This is a \emph{different operating regime} from the Day~5 identification, which was carried out with the robot rolling on the floor. Any direct comparison between the Test~0 steady-state voltage and the Day~5 DC-gain prediction would therefore be an apples-to-oranges comparison --- they describe different physical conditions.

\paragraph{What the Test~0 steady-state voltage actually measures.} With the wheels free in the air, the motor torque only has to overcome internal drivetrain friction and the back-EMF produced by rotation. Using the REGBOT motor and drivetrain parameters (gear ratio $N_G = 9.69$, wheel radius $W_R = 0.03\,\mathrm{m}$, EMF constant $K_{\mathrm{emf}} = 0.0105\,\mathrm{V\,s/rad}$, armature resistance $R_A = 1.65\,\Omega$):
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{\mathrm{motor}} = \frac{v}{W_R}\,N_G = \frac{0.3}{0.03}\cdot 9.69 = 96.9\,\mathrm{rad/s},
    \quad
    V_{\mathrm{BEMF}} = K_{\mathrm{emf}}\,\omega_{\mathrm{motor}} \approx 1.02\,\mathrm{V}.
\end{equation}
The remaining $\approx 0.8\,\mathrm{V}$ of the measured $1.8\,\mathrm{V}$ steady-state is accounted for by the $I\!R$ voltage drop across the armature resistance due to the current required to overcome bearing and gearbox friction. The observation is consistent with the wheels-up regime and does \emph{not} indicate that the Day~5 on-floor model is inaccurate.

A direct test of the Day~5 model would have required an on-floor open-loop run with balance disabled, which would need training wheels or a similar stabilising fixture and was not part of the test plan. The on-floor behaviour is instead validated \emph{indirectly} by the Tasks~2--4 experiments: in all three the robot drives on the floor with the wheel-speed PI operating inside the cascade, and each test meets its specification.

\paragraph{Rise time and log-sampling caveat.} The measured $90\,\%$ rise time of $0.329\,\mathrm{s}$ is slower than the $\approx 80\,\mathrm{ms}$ predicted for a first-order closed loop at $\omega_c = 30\,\mathrm{rad/s}$. The logged velocity estimate lags by $\approx 5\,\mathrm{ms}$ (reported in the log header), the log interval is $15\,\mathrm{ms}$ while the internal controller runs at $1\,\mathrm{kHz}$, and the wheels-up load is very light --- all of which affect the transient seen in the plot without necessarily indicating a slower true closed-loop bandwidth. Crucially, Task~1 has a $121.6^{\circ}$ phase-margin cushion, so even a noticeably reduced effective bandwidth would not threaten stability. The outer loops (Tasks~2--4) are designed against $G_{tilt}$ and $G_{vel,\mathrm{outer}}$, obtained by \texttt{linearize()} on the \emph{full} Simulink model with the Task~1 gains in place, not against $G_{vel,\mathrm{day5}}$ directly, so any residual Day~5 error propagates only weakly to the outer designs.

The left and right motors agree to $0.36\,\%$ in mean velocity, so no per-wheel gain trim is needed.
